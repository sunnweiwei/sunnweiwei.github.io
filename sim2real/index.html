Abstract

Why Should We Care About the Sim2Real Gap?

As NLP evaluation shifts from static benchmarks to multi-turn interactive settings, LLM-based simulators have become the dominant approach to creating user proxies — both for generating conversation turns and providing evaluation signals. Yet their faithfulness to real human behavior is rarely verified.

We formalize the Sim2Real gap in user simulation and present the first study running the full τ-bench protocol with real humans (451 participants, 165 tasks), benchmarking 31 LLM simulators across proprietary, open-source, and specialized families using USI — a metric we introduce to quantify how well LLM simulators resemble real user interactive behaviors and feedback.

LLM simulators are excessively cooperative, stylistically uniform, and lack realistic frustration or ambiguity, creating an "easy mode" that inflates agent success rates above the human baseline. Rule-based rewards are similarly misaligned, with 70.6% of automatically failed interactions judged successful by the humans who completed them.
Research Questions

Three Lenses on the Gap

01
Behavioral Fidelity
Do simulated users behave like real people — in how they phrase requests, reveal information, and react to errors? Divergence here risks optimizing agents toward the wrong goals.
02
Evaluative Alignment
Do simulated evaluations provide the same quality signals as real humans? Misalignment here means benchmarks misrepresent true agent quality across multiple dimensions.
03
Rule-Based Rewards
Can automatic binary rewards substitute for human feedback? Many benchmarks rely solely on exact state-match rewards that may not capture what users actually care about.
Framework

A Taxonomy of Sim2Real Gaps

We decompose the gap into behavioral dimensions (how simulators interact) and evaluative dimensions (how they judge quality). Each dimension is grounded in established theory.

Key Findings

What We Discovered

🎭
Simulators Create an "Easy Mode"
Most general-purpose LLM simulators yield higher agent success rates than the human baseline of 63.6%, with top models reaching 77.8%. Agents appear more capable than they truly are when evaluated with LLM users.
📊
LLM Evaluators Are Systematically Biased
GPT-5.1 overestimates AI human-likeness by 55% of the rating scale and overall score by 18%, while simultaneously underestimating task success. This asymmetric bias disqualifies LLMs as reliable quality proxies.
⚠️
Binary Rewards Are Orthogonal to User Perception
70.6% of reward=0 interactions are judged successful by the real humans who completed them. Exact database-state matching catches discrepancies that users never notice during the interaction.
🔗
Capability ≠ Simulation Fidelity
Higher general model capability does not reliably translate to more faithful user simulation — except within the GPT family (r=0.91). Claude (r=0.48) and Gemini (r=−0.52) show no significant correlation.

Takeaways

What the Community Should Do

Our findings do not invalidate LLM-as-user benchmarks — they remain valuable for rapid, reproducible agent development. Rather, we argue the community should mind the Sim2Real gap:

1. Acknowledge limited applicability — simulation fidelity varies by domain, task type, and user persona. Do not treat simulator results as ground truth.

2. Measure the gap systematically — use human validation studies periodically, especially before claiming state-of-the-art performance on agent benchmarks.

3. Design evaluation accounting for simulator gaps — report USI alongside agent performance, and use hybrid metrics that combine simulated scale with human calibration.

4. Build better user simulators — future work should target the specific behavioral gaps identified here: realistic frustration, incremental information release, and non-cooperative error reactions.
